Music Primer

This topic is intended to be a very basic introduction to the music grand staff, and how it is used in the WinAGI Sound Editor. Keep in mind it was written by someone who has had no formal training in music. But hopefully, it is reasonably accurate.

If you need additional help understanding music notation, there are many online resources available. Use your favorite search engine to find answers to any questions you may have.

The Grand Staff

WinAGI uses the grand staff to display music notes. The grand staff includes an upper staff where notes above middle C are normally displayed, and a lower staff where notes below middle C are normally displayed.

Middle C is the note that is approximately in the middle of a regular 88 key piano. In WinAGI, middle C is assigned to octave 4.

On the grand staff, middle C is located one full line below the bottom of the treble staff. It is also located one full line above the top line of the bass staff.

In AGI, the lowest possible note is note A in the second octave. The highest note is note G in the ninth octave.

The curly ampersand-like symbol on the treble staff is called the treble clef. The backwards C shaped symbol on the bass staff is called the bass clef.

Measures and Time Signature

Notes are divided into equal lengths of times, called measures. Measures are marked on the staves by a vertical line.

The time signature defines the amount and type of notes that each measure contains, and consists of two numbers. The first number represents the number of notes per measure. The second number represents the the size of each beat (4 means each beat is a quarter note, 8 means each beat is an eighth note, etc.)

The two most common time signatures are 4:4 and 3:4. (There are many other time signatures, but this primer won't address them.)

In WinAGI, there is not a practical use for time signatures, so all music is drawn as if it were in 4:4 time. The time signature would normally be drawn on the staff right after the clef. WinAGI doesn't draw it since it doesn't provide any usefulness.

Accidentals

There are twelve notes in an octave, but only eight of them are represented by letters. They correspond to the white keys on a piano keyboard and are called natural notes. The other five notes in an octave correspond to the black keys and are called sharp or flat, depending on which direction you read them from.

Sharp notes are natural notes that are one step higher:

 

Flat notes are natural notes that are one step lower:

 

For example, the black key between notes C and D can be called either C sharp (C#) or D flat (Db):

  These refer to the same note.

To represent sharps and flats on the staff, you include the sharp or flat note directly in front of the note. The marks are called accidentals.

Keys

There are lots of different keys that music can be written in. Keys are represented by including combinations of sharp or flat marks at the beginning of the staff

By using keys, you don't have to include the accidental marks in front of every note. Any note on a line or space that the key already has marked as flat or sharp is assumed to be flat or sharp as well.

Rests
